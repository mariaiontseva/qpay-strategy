Hybrid and all-in-one

04 competitors
01

Countingup

↗
Hybrid

"Money, accounting and tax in one smart app." The tightest product-thesis match to QPay — and the clearest cautionary tale of what happens when you aim at sole traders instead of Ltds.

Pricing
£3 · £9 · £18/mo (tiered by deposits)
Scale (real, not marketing)
34K paying · $3.1M rev 2024 · stuck at Series A since 2020
Strengths
  • Only UK product that collapses banking + books + tax into a single ledger — no data re-entry, no reconciliation
  • SmartTax AI real-time tax estimates; free HMRC VAT and Self Assessment filing
  • Bundled Dojo Go card reader (RRP £179) and 27K+ PayPoint cash deposit locations
  • Accountant Hub read-only access for practices — same distribution model Q Accountants would use
Weaknesses — the real picture
  • Growth has stalled. 34K paying customers (Latka 2024) vs the "100,000 small businesses" marketing line — website figure is cumulative / ever-registered, not active. Sensor Tower download trend confirms new acquisition is thin.
  • Revenue tiny: $3.1M in 2024 (from $2.1M in 2023). ANNA does ~$38M ARR at the same headcount bracket. Countingup is an order of magnitude smaller despite same vintage.
  • Capital starved: Still at Series A (£9.1M, March 2020). Five years of trying, no Series B. Total raised $21.4M across 4 rounds and 28 investors — the small-round-many-investors pattern of a company that cannot close a priced B.
  • Likely quiet headcount cut: Latka logged 52 staff in 2024; Tracxn shows 41 by Feb 2026 — roughly 20% reduction without a press release.
  • App Store reviews polarising: Trustpilot "Excellent" on top-level, but App Store over the last 12 months shows sparse 5★ against a stream of 1★ — account freezes with poor comms, demands for client bank statements flagged as privacy breaches, no phone support, no Apple/Google Pay, no desktop.
  • Built for sole traders, by design: no dividend workflow, no cap table, no board resolutions, no PSC filings, no multicurrency, no team cards, no payroll, no Companies House integration. They cannot pivot to Ltds without rebuilding the product.
QPay angle Countingup's real importance is as a validator, not a threat. They proved there is real demand for "one app = account + books + tax" and got 34K UK businesses to pay for it — but stalled at $3M revenue, cannot raise a B, appear to be quietly shrinking, and never touched Ltd governance. The category is proven, the incumbent is weakening, the top half of the market (Ltds with dividends and shareholders) is untouched. QPay takes the same bundle up-market to UK Ltds, anchors distribution on Q Accountants' existing client base, and wins on operational quality (support, CASS-compliant safeguarding, no silent freezes) in a segment Countingup structurally cannot reach.
