Glossary — the acronyms first

Before the table, here is what the jargon actually means in plain English.

KYC
Know Your Customer. Legal duty for a bank to verify who its customers really are before giving them an account. Usually means: government ID + selfie + a few personal data points.
AML
Anti-Money Laundering. Rules that force banks to detect and report dirty money. KYC is the first step; ongoing monitoring of transactions is the second.
PEP
Politically Exposed Person. A senior politician, judge, military officer, their family or close associates. Banks must apply extra scrutiny to PEPs because of bribery / corruption risk.
Sanctions screening
Checking the customer's name against government black-lists (UN, OFAC, EU, UK HMT). If there is a hit, the bank cannot open the account.
Adverse media
Automated press search for negative news about the customer (fraud, crime, regulatory action). Required by most regulators as part of enhanced due diligence.
Liveness detection
Tech that proves the selfie is a real, live human in front of the camera — not a photo, video, mask or deep-fake. Active liveness asks the user to do something (turn head, smile). Passive liveness analyses pixels silently.
PAD & ISO 30107-3
Presentation Attack Detection. The international standard that measures liveness quality. Independent labs (mostly iBeta) certify vendors at Level 1 (basic photo / video attacks) or Level 2 (3D masks, advanced spoofs). Level 2 is the bar regulators expect for banks.
eIDAS / ETSI
European regulation (eIDAS) and the technical standards body that implements it (ETSI). An eIDAS-certified vendor can produce identity proofs with the same legal weight as a wet signature across the EU.
QES
Qualified Electronic Signature. The strongest electronic signature in the EU, legally equivalent to handwritten. Some IDV vendors (e.g. Onfido/Entrust) issue QES after the KYC step.
SOC 2 / ISO 27001
Security audits. SOC 2 Type II is the US standard, ISO 27001 the international one. Both prove the vendor has documented controls over data, access and incidents. Mandatory for any bank vendor.
KYB
Know Your Business. Same idea as KYC but for company customers — checking incorporation, ultimate beneficial owners, directors.
Pass rate
Share of users who complete the KYC flow successfully on first try. A 95% pass rate means 5 in 100 customers fail and you lose them — directly tied to onboarding economics.

Ranking for a bank

  1. Onfido (Entrust) — best for a UK/EU regulated bank. Tier-1 bank pedigree, eIDAS + QES, FCA-recognised. Worth the opaque pricing if you are pursuing a banking licence or facing regulator scrutiny.
  2. Sumsub — best price-performance with public pricing. ETSI eIDAS, AML built in, Level 2 PAD. Pick if you want to launch fast without a procurement cycle.
  3. Jumio — best for high-volume regulated banks (> 100k/year). Strongest banking customer book. Costs only become competitive at scale.
  4. Veriff — best for fastest UX (~6 s decision). Pick if onboarding drop-off is your main problem.
  5. iDenfy — cheapest, pay-per-approval economics. Pick as a second-source vendor or for cost-sensitive customer segments.
  6. Persona — best workflow / orchestration flexibility. Strong US fintech fit, lighter EU bank presence.
  7. Trulioo — best for global breadth and data-based KYC (emerging-market customers without strong ID documents). Usually paired with a biometric specialist.
  8. Stripe Identity — best for fastest dev integration and transparent low-volume pricing. Not standalone bank-grade (no AML, no eIDAS, no published PAD level). Use as a complement, not the primary KYC.

Top 3 recommendations for a bank

Stripe Identity, Persona and Trulioo are excluded — none are bank-grade as a sole KYC supplier (Stripe has no AML / no eIDAS / no published PAD level; Persona is US-fintech-shaped; Trulioo is a data layer, not a biometric specialist). The three below are real candidates.

#1 · Default safe choice
Onfido (Entrust)

The vendor that UK / EU neobanks already picked: Revolut, Monzo, Bunq, Tide, ClearBank. eIDAS-certified with QES — only one in this list. FCA-recognised. Best when the bank will face regulator scrutiny or pursue a banking licence. Trade-off: no published price, mid-speed (~30–60 s), enterprise-only contracting.

#2 · Best price-performance
Sumsub

Public price $1.85 per verification with AML / PEP / adverse media built in — no separate sanctions vendor needed. ETSI EN 319 411-1 (eIDAS-compatible), iBeta Level 2 PAD. 14,500 documents, 220 countries. Best when speed-to-launch matters and you want to skip the procurement cycle. Trade-off: customer base is heavily crypto-bank, less prestige in classical EU retail banking.

#3 · Best at high volume
Jumio

The strongest tier-1 banking book in the market: HSBC, Standard Chartered, Lloyds, BBVA, ING, Wise. Both Level 1 and Level 2 PAD certified. NIST 800-63 IAL2/AAL2-aligned, FedRAMP-ready. Best at > 100k verifications/year — price drops to $0.75–$2 at that scale. Trade-off: most expensive at low volumes ($3–$8), opaque pricing, heaviest enterprise contracting.

How to choose between them: regulator-first & banking licence → Onfido. Speed-to-launch + transparent budget → Sumsub. High volume & existing tier-1 bank infrastructure → Jumio.
